Toolkit

We brainstorm the following ideas using cutting-edge NLP techniques that have not been adopted in automated fact-checking. Each idea is displayed with a text-based storyboard and a wizard of oz prototype. Please read the storyboard description to understand how NLP is adopted to assist fact-checking and explore prototypes to make sense of its implementations followed by instructions.

1. Prepare stand-alone claims before fact-checking

Description: this idea aims to create a stand-alone claim by replacing pronouns, removing discourse markers, and adding local conditions to decontextualize it from the paragraph.

Objective: this idea aims to understand how useful to have stand-alone claim prepared before checking claims.

"Austria is expecting to declare nationwide lockdown of unvaccinated people, in what would be the broadest restrictions that any country has yet placed on those who have opted not to get the jab."

2. Help fact-checkers investigate ambiguous terms in a claim

Description: this idea aims to detect ambiguous terms in a claim and help fact-checkers generate contextual questions for further investigations based on these terms.

Objective: this idea aims to lead the discussion of how to objectively quantify claims' ambiguity for fact-checking, including finding ambiguous terms or analyzing a complex claim.

Prototype

Please enter paragraphs or select one example below:

Examples:

"People in the Muslim Brotherhood openly stated they want to declare war on Israel and they would end the peace agreement with Israel and they would work towards instituting something we told you about, a caliphate."

"Austria is expecting to declare nationwide lockdown of unvaccinated people, in what would be the broadest restrictions that any country has yet placed on those who have opted not to get the jab."
  1. Highlight ambiguous terms
  2. Generate contextual questions from these terms

3. Retrieve quantitative related information from analytical queries

Description: this idea aims to help fact-checkers search quantitative related information including place, time, and statistics. It also generates a final answer by combining these quantitative information.

Objective: this idea aims to lead the discussion of how machine can help to assist fact-checkers in checking quantitative-related claims.

Prototype

Please enter a sentence that contains analytical keywords such as space and time, or select one example below:

Examples:

How many total covid cases India have reported from April 7th to April 9th

GDP of China in recent 3 years

Monthly income for people living in Florida
  1. Detect analytical keywords from the search query, including time, space, and search objects
  2. Retrieve quantitative relevant information
  3. Generate final answers from retrieval information

4. Review argumentation in the fact-checking report

Description: this idea aims to assist fact-checkers to evaluate the argumentation in a fact-checking report from three perspectives, including readability, coherency, and persuasiveness.

Objective: this idea aims to lead the discussion of how machine can assist fact-checkers to review their reports, not only about the argumentations, but also other criteria that fact-checkers can objectively quantify from their writing experience.

Prototype

Please enter one paragraph or select one from the fact-check below:

Example:

Claim: The push for high-speed internet for rural areas, from the start, “was a Democratic thing.”

Wisconsin Democrats did prioritize boosting rural broadband early on — particularly during the Obama administration, when then-Gov. Jim Doyle assigned nearly $23 million in federal funds to fix problems.
Under Gov. Scott Walker, who took control in 2011, Republicans sent that money back to the federal government, saying it had too many strings attached.
Wisconsin Republicans say they took an early aim at the issue by advocating to remove the sales tax on internet access, which could ease the burden on small internet service providers.

  1. Highlight claims and premises
  2. Score readability, coherency, and persuasiveness
