We brainstorm the following ideas using cutting-edge NLP techniques that have not been adopted in automated fact-checking. Each idea is displayed with a text-based storyboard and a wizard of oz prototype. Please read the storyboard description to understand how NLP is adopted to assist fact-checking and explore prototypes to make sense of its implementations followed by instructions.

1. Prepare stand-alone claims before fact-checking

Description: this idea aims to create a stand-alone claim by replacing pronouns, removing discourse markers, and adding local conditions to decontextualize it from the paragraph.

Objective: this idea aims to understand how useful to have stand-alone claim prepared before checking claims.

2. Help fact-checkers investigate ambiguous terms in a claim

Description: this idea aims to detect ambiguous terms in a claim and help fact-checkers generate contextual questions for further investigations based on these terms.

Objective: this idea aims to lead the discussion of how to objectively quantify claims' ambiguity for fact-checking, and how the detection system can be situated in the workflow of human fact-checkers.

Prototype

Please enter one sentence or select one examples below:

Examples:

"People in the Muslim Brotherhood openly stated they want to declare war on Israel and they would end the peace agreement with Israel and they would work towards instituting something we told you about, a caliphate."

"Austria is expecting to declare nationwide lockdown of unvaccinated people, in what would be the broadest restrictions that any country has yet placed on those who have opted not to get the jab."
  1. Highlight ambiguous terms
  2. Generate contextual questions from these terms

3. Retrieve quantitative related information from analytical queries

Description: this idea aims to help fact-checkers search quantitative related information including place, time, and statistics. It also generates a final answer by combining these quantitative information.

Objective: this idea aims to lead the discussion of how machine can help to assist fact-checkers in checking quantitative-related claims.

Prototype

Examples:

Monthly income for people living in Florida
  1. Detect analytical keywords from the search query, including time, space, and search objects
  2. Retrieve quantitative relevant information
  3. Generate final answers from retrieval information

4. Identify the weak premise in arguments

Description: this idea aims to assist fact-checkers to identify the weak premise when reading an article. Potential weak premises contain the argument is not supported by evidence or the statement is extravagant or absolute.

Objective: this idea aims to lead the discussion of how machine can assist fact-checkers to review people's arguments, including what criteria fact-checkers use to review arguments and how to objectively quantify these criteria from past experience.

Prototype

Please enter one paragraph or select one examples below:

Examples:

The last Presidential election (2016) and most succeeding elections have proven that elections are more about party affiliations than actual views or the character of the individual being elected. In one of the most extreme examples, Roy Moore was backed by the Republican Party even though he was accused of sexual misconduct and sexual assault of minors simply because he was a Republican. This also allows voters to be lazy, as many will simply vote for their party without researching the values and character of the person they are voting for.

Our Congress is slow an inefficient because Democrats and Republicans are more focused on opposing one another than they are on developing actual solutions to issues like gun control and abortion. It is the job of elected officials to represent ALL of the people of their district/state/country, not just the people that voted for them or agree with them, and following the ideals of a political party does not allow for this. Political parties force us to think in terms of black and white, and this is both inefficient and inappropriate for issues that affect the entire country.

Many young voters do not think this way--many Americans are becoming disenfranchised with the entire political system. This is an outdated system, and either needs to adapt or change completely to better fit the needs of the people.
  1. Categorize different types of weak premise
  2. Identify weak premise in arguments and opinions
